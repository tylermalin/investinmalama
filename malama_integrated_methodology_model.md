## SECTION 1: BIOCHAR METHODOLOGY

### 1.1 Overview & Science

**What is Biochar?**
Biochar is carbon-rich, porous charcoal produced by pyrolysis of biomass (wood, agricultural waste) in oxygen-limited conditions. It sequesters carbon long-term (100–1,000+ year residence time) and improves soil health.

**Carbon Pathway:**
1. **Feedstock C:** Biomass (wood chips, straw, nutshells) = 45–50% carbon by dry weight
2. **Pyrolysis:** Heat biomass to 400–800°C without oxygen → Biochar (35–40% yield) + Bio-oil + Syngas
3. **Sequestration:** Biochar applied to soil; carbon remains stable for centuries (90%+ permanence)
4. **Co-benefits:** Improved water retention (+10–20% soil moisture), microbial habitat, nutrient cycling

**MRV Approach (Puro Biochar v3.0 Protocol):**
- Mass balance: Feedstock weight → Biochar yield → Application rate (GPS/load cell proof)
- Carbon content: Lab analysis (elemental C, H, N, O via CHNS combustion)
- Permanence: Stability assay (H₂O₂ oxidation test, expected <5% loss over 100 years)
- Co-benefits: KANU attestation (soil health, crop yield gains)

### 2.1 Enhanced Weathering via Nutrient Optimization

**Challenge:** Standard alfalfa fertilization (if needed) uses synthetic N-P-K, which adds ~0.5–1.0 tCO₂e/acre from production & transport.

**Solution:** Integrate **nitrogenous fertilizer sequestration** (urea ammonium nitrate, UAN) as proxy for CO₂ savings via:
1. **Reduced synthetic N input** (basalt weathering supplies Mg, Ca; lower N demand)
2. **Microbial respiration bonus** (enhanced microbial activity → soil C accumulation)
3. **N₂O emission reduction** (proper pH buffering from basalt → lower nitrification rates)
